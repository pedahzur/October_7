# The Few Who Came Unbidden

On the morning of October 7, 2023, a young man named Aner Shapira stood in a concrete bus shelter on Route 232, near Kibbutz Re'im. Around him crouched men and women who had fled a music festival an hour before. Grenades came through the doorway. Shapira picked them up and threw them back. He threw back seven, or eight, the survivors lost count, before one killed him. He had served in a special unit. No one ordered him to that shelter. He was there because he had run toward the sound.

I open with Shapira because his choice is the subject of this essay. The story of October 7 is told as a failure, and it was one. The fence broke in three hours. The army reached the worst-hit kibbutzim late. But a second story runs underneath the first, and the numbers now let me tell it. Israel did not lose the day. It did not lose the day because thousands of its citizens did what Shapira did. They came unbidden.

A research team built a database of the security personnel killed that day. Each entry carries one small flag: did this person hold a post, or did they come to the fight on their own. I re-ran the analysis from the raw file, and the pattern is sharp. One group had been standing their posts when the assault began. Seventy of every hundred were conscripts, the youngest soldiers, holding the line they were placed on. A second group had no post to hold. They heard the reports, took their weapons, and drove south. Among these, the conscripts almost vanish. Eight in a hundred. The rest were professionals and reservists, men and women who had served, and who chose the fight a second time.

Inside that second group sat the special units. I want to state their weight with care. Among the fallen, a fighter from the special forces was seven times more likely than another to have come unbidden. The figure holds after I adjust for rank and length of service. Reservists carried an even greater weight. Conscripts the opposite. Officer rank, on its own, did nothing. This is the mechanism. The men and women trained for the hardest missions were the ones who reached for the fight without an order.

They paid for the distance. The ones who came unbidden died later in the day than the ones at their posts, a median of six hours against four. The gap is not a detail. It is the road. They scrambled within ninety minutes, much of it on their own initiative, and then they fought south through ambushes to reach the communities that needed them. Sayeret Matkal and Duvdevan set out fast and reached the gate of Kfar Aza at half past ten. The frogmen of Shayetet 13 reached the Sufa outpost at half past two and fought until evening to free 250 soldiers trapped in a cafeteria. Where no one reached at all, the cost was complete. At Nir Oz, which no unit reached in time, the attackers took 75 people alive.

Now I want to widen the frame. Where does a reflex like this come from. A society does not improvise it on a single morning. I argue that October 7 revealed a tradition built over a century. In 1920 a small faction in the Yishuv lost an argument. It held that safety required a secret, professional, offensive corps, not a citizen militia waiting behind a fence. The argument lost the vote. It did not die. It went underground, into a clandestine layer beneath the legal community, and it returned in every generation in a new uniform. The Clandestine Kibbutz of 1921. The Special Night Squads of 1938. The Palmach of 1941. Sayeret Matkal of 1957. The undercover units of the late 1980s. Each carried forward a temper more than a tactic: lead from the front, prize initiative over conformity, fight for a cause you can name. That temper is what answered on October 7.

I do not claim the data proves the century. The database records the dead, not all who fought, and it cannot reach the living who turned the day and walked away. The over-representation I measure is over-representation among the fallen. The inference to the whole rests on a proxy, and I mark it as one. Nor was every elite unit alike. One waited for orders while others moved on their own. The picture is not a monument. It is evidence, and it points in one direction.

Aner Shapira did not know this history when he stood in the shelter. He did not need to. The tradition had done its work before the morning began. It had selected him, trained him, and set in him a disposition that decided what he would do when the grenades came through the door. The few carried the day. The record of the dead shows who the few were. And it shows that a small society, a hundred years earlier, had chosen to build its strength in such people. That choice is the subject of this book.
