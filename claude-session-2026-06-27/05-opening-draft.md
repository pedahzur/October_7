# October 7

On Saturday, October 7, 2023, at 06:29, the Red Alert sirens opened across the Gaza Envelope. The sound was familiar. The Negev had heard it for twenty years. This time the rockets were a screen. Behind them, at about 24 points along a 40-mile fence, Hamas squads waited to cross. At 06:30 the fence broke. By day's end the attackers had breached it 119 times.

What broke with it was an idea. Israel had built its southern defense as a line. A fence, sensors, observation posts, a division to hold them, and behind that a country that trusted the line to hold. For a few hours the line did not hold. Paragliders came down on Kfar Aza and Netiv HaAsara. A fast boat landed at Zikim. Gunmen took Erez Crossing by ten to seven and the Nahal Oz base soon after. They entered Sderot and killed 13 pensioners at a bus stop. They overran the Nova festival and hunted the people who fled it across the open fields. They went house to house through Be'eri. By nine in the morning the killing and the taking of hostages ran across the whole envelope, and the state's front line was gone.

I want to begin the book here, at the lowest point, because of what happened next. The line did not turn the day. Nor did the units whose names fill this book, not in its first and worst hours. Many of them scrambled fast. Fighters from Sayeret Matkal, from Duvdevan, from the rescue and reconnaissance units set out within ninety minutes of the first reports, much of it on their own initiative, before an order reached them. But scrambling was not arriving. Sayeret Matkal and Duvdevan reached the gate of Kfar Aza only at half past ten, four hours in. Shayetet 13 reached the Sufa outpost at half past two and fought until evening to free 250 soldiers trapped in a cafeteria. Shaldag reached Holit at half past three. The road to the worst-hit communities ran through ambushes, and the cost of crossing it was hours. The elite of the Israeli military arrived in pieces, and it arrived late to the places that needed it most.

The turn came from below. It came from the civilian squads of the kibbutzim, men and women with light weapons who met the first wave at their own gates. It came from off-duty soldiers and reservists and police who heard the sirens, took their rifles, and drove south on their own authority toward the sound. Aner Shapira stood in a roadside shelter near Re'im and threw grenades back out of it until one killed him. Yosef Guedalia died pulling civilians out of Kfar Aza. Benjamin Trakerniski, a major in the armored corps on his day off, was killed carrying the wounded out of Be'eri. None of them waited for an order. By nightfall, community by community, this scattered and unbidden force had retaken most of the ground the state had lost by nine that morning. Where no armed response came at all, the cost was complete. At Nir Oz, which no unit reached in time, the attackers took 75 people alive, more than from any other place.

This is not impression. A database of the security personnel killed that day records, for each, whether the person held a post or came to the fight unbidden. Of those who came unbidden, one in three belonged to a special unit. Among those who fell at their posts, the figure was one in twenty. Adjust for rank and length of service, and a fighter from the special units was seven times more likely than the rest to have joined the battle on his own. The wave that came unbidden was made of professionals and reservists, not conscripts.

This is the fact the book sets out to explain. A modern state lost its front line in three hours. It did not lose the day. It did not lose the day because thousands of its citizens carried a way of fighting in their own bodies, and they reached for it without being told. Many had passed through the special units, or through the reserves those units shaped, or through a national culture those units had marked. They led from the front. They moved toward the fire. They acted on their own judgment in the absence of command. These are not accidents of character. They are the residue of a tradition, built over a century, that taught a small society to trust the initiative of the few over the mass of the many.

I do not offer October 7 as a victory. It was a catastrophe, and the book will not soften it. I offer it as evidence of a pattern. The pattern is that Israel, from its first decades, chose to build strength in small, mobile, self-directed units, and to seed their habits through the wider force and the wider society. On the worst day in the country's history, that choice was the thing that answered when the line broke. How a fugitive idea of 1920, carried underground by a defeated faction, became the reflex of a nation under attack in 2023, is the question this book sets out to answer.
